Finding Wine-Food Pairings with A Big Data Approach for Wine-Searcher

When & Where

2023.09 - 2023.10
KTH Royal Institute of Technology

Members

Veerle Uhl
Yuan Chen
Yuxuan Zhang

Description

This project aims to use big data and machine learning methods to uncover potential patterns in wine and food pairing based on the existing database of wine and food pairings derived from user search frequencies on the Wine-Searcher website. In the initial stages of the project, extensive research was conducted. It was found that in the eyes of wine culture enthusiasts and wine connoisseurs, the primary considerations when pairing wine with food are six taste features: sweet, acid, salt, piquant, fat, and bitter. However, databases that contain both these taste features and the relationships between wine and food pairings are not commonly available. Therefore, the decision was made to extract these six taste features from wine and food reviews in online marketplaces and then connect them with the wine and food pairing database on the Wine-Searcher website, thus creating a supervised learning dataset. As a result, all the content in our dataset comes from the subjective feedback data of numerous website users with diverse preferences for wine and food. We aim to explore whether machine learning can uncover patterns within this data and provide new insights for traditional wine and food pairing and wine tasting methods. Feel free to explore our GitHub repo to see our code implementation.

My Work

In this project, my responsibilities included data collection and cleaning, exploratory data analysis (EDA) and feature engineering, ensemble learning models construction, and model performance evaluation and visualization. To begin, I utilized Beautiful Soup to scrape approximately 100,000 wine and food pairing records from the Wine-Searcher website. Additionally, I sourced two datasets from Kaggle related to wine reviews and food reviews, which contained roughly 140,000 records and 500,000 records, respectively. In processing the reviews datasets, duplicates, HTML tags, mentions, URLs, punctuations, and English stop words in nltk.corpus were dropped. Additionally, the reviews were tokenized into sentences and then into words, and after that stemmed by the Snowball Stemmer. The cleaned words were then mapped to the predefined thesaurus, which are visualized in the following word clouds (the left one is for wine and the right wine is for food).

To extract the six taste features from the reviews, a Word2Vec model with IDF weighting was employed to calculate 300-dimensional document vectors, which were subsequently reduced to one dimension using PCA. As a result, for each wine or food review data record (corresponding to each wine or food type), there was a one-dimensional value representing the six taste features. After that, I integrated textual information about wine styles from both the wine and food pairings dataset and the wine reviews dataset. Using Jaccard similarity in combination with 2-grams, these datasets were linked together to form our training and testing sets. By analyzing the distribution relationships of the extracted features, I also introduced the DBSCAN clustering results as supplementary features to enrich the data representation. Furthermore, I utilized wine and food pairing rules formulated by wine connoisseurs to perform the final filtering of the dataset, which resulted in a dataset more likely to reveal underlying patterns.

During the model training and evaluation phase, my team members and I tested various models’ performances and ultimately selected a comprehensive ensemble learning model, combining Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, AdaBoost, and XGBoost, using soft voting to obtain the best results. I created both classification and regression models. The classification model predicts the food category that best matches a given wine style, while the regression model predicts the taste features of recommended foods based on the input wine style. I primarily optimized the model performance through grid search, considering metrics such as accuracy, ROC, AUC, RMSE, and others. Finally, I introduced a radar chart as a visualization method for the regression model's results, as shown in the following figure.

Complexity in music perception - is it reflected in listeners’ pupil dilation?

When & Where

2022.09 - 2022.10
KTH Royal Institute of Technology

Members

Edris Hakimzada
Helena T. Linder Miñambres
Izabelle Lundqvist
Yuxuan Zhang

Description

This study aimed to investigate whether pupil dilation of music listeners reflects their perception of music complexity. We recruited eight participants and used the Tobii Pro Nano eye tracker to capture participants' pupil diameter data while they listened to music of different complexity. The conclusions were obtained and presented through data processing based on Python. Feel free to explore my GitHub repo to see the detailed code for the data analysis part.

My Work

In this study, my main responsibilities involved the experimental setup using the PsychoPy package and data cleaning, analysis, and visualization using Python (pandas, matplotlib, seaborn, etc). I also applied a two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) to test the significance of our resulted effects. Before the experiment, I implemented the overall logic of the experiment using the PsychoPy package, including the music playing, data recording, interface for connecting the eye tracker, and the questionnaires for the participants. Our final experimental setup is shown in the following photo.

The eye tracker sampled data at a frequency of 60 Hz, resulting in over 50,000 data points recorded for each participant. With these, I applied various methods to perform data cleaning on a total of nearly 500,000 data. For instance, I classified the data based on music complexity and participants' liking level of music. I also calculated the percentage change in pupil diameter data to highlight significant variations in pupil diameters. Additionally, I handled missing values by applying linear interpolation. After completing the data cleaning, I consolidated the data of all participants and performed polynomial regression on it. The relationship between changes in pupil diameter and music complexity, with a 95% confidence interval and an order of 10 was plotted as shown in the image below. Therefore, we drew a preliminary conclusion that pupil dilation might be induced more by more complex music.

Besides, I also generated a plot illustrating the relationship between changes in pupil diameter and participants' liking levels of music. Once again, a 95% confidence interval and an order of 10 were included, as depicted in the image below. We then reached another preliminary conclusion that the music that was either more liked or disliked by the participants appeared to elicit larger pupil dilation.

In addition to examining the effects of music complexity and participants' liking levels on pupil dilation separately, it is also important to consider the interaction effect of these two factors. Therefore, I created an interaction effect plot utilizing the estimated marginal means in Jamovi, as illustrated in the image below, showcasing the effects that each liking level of music has on pupil dilation under all three music complexity levels.

Members

Description

This project is my undergraduate capstone design. It established an integrated web system for evaluating CO2 emissions from buildings. This system includes a coefficient database, an integrated model for assessing building carbon emissions, and a user-friendly web interface. Users can obtain building life-cycle carbon emission data by inputting estimation requests and relevant parameters, and they can also view real-time carbon emission data based on simulated results. Here is a introduction video of this project.

My Work

In this project, my main responsibilities included establishing the building carbon emission evaluation model in accordance with the GB/T51366-2019 Chinese standard, and designing the coefficient database as a relational database. Strict adherence to the Quality Function Deployment (QFD) methodology was employed for product design and development in this project. The following diagram represents the House of Quality (HoQ) for this project, illustrating the relationships between customer requirements and product features.

The building carbon emission estimation model was developed to encompass both life-cycle and real-time aspects. The life-cycle carbon emission evaluation model drew inspiration from the formulas outlined in GB/T51366-2019 and was divided into four stages: building materials production and transportation, construction, operation, and demolition. The real-time carbon emission evaluation model was derived from the life-cycle operation stage model. The diagram below illustrates the relationships between the stages of the model and the required carbon emission coefficients.

Based on the designed evaluation model, the coefficient database was crafted as a relational database adhering to the Third Normal Form (3NF) to reduce data redundancy and enhance system performance. The following illustration presents the Entity-Relationship (ER) diagram of the database. It can be seen that the carbon emission coefficients of building materials, transportation methods, and consumed energy were organized into different tables separately, and the carbon emission factors of building materials and transportation methods were related with the key of unit. In this way, the repeating group issue of the consumed energy was avoided. Besides, the energy carbon emission factors and the energy consumption of machinery were related with the key of energy type to meet the Second Normal Form.

In addition, I also contributed to the setup of the SQLite database within the Django framework, and the construction of a web user interface compliant with the WCAG 2.0 standard. This effort resulted in the final web system, which consists of a homepage, a life-cycle carbon emission estimator page, a real-time carbon emission estimator page, a database page, and an admin page. The system demo is shown in the previous introduction video.

Housing Price Prediction Based on CCAO Illinois Housing Sales Information Dataset

When & Where

2021.09 - 2021.12
Shanghai Jiao Tong University

Individual Project

What I've Done

This project worked with the dataset comes from the Cook County Assessor’s Office (CCAO) in Illinois, a government institution that determines property taxes across most of Chicago’s metropolitan area and its nearby suburbs. New records are still coming into the CCAO dataset every week! In this project, 300,000 records with 61 features were extracted from the CCAO dataset to create the training and test sets.

Firstly, I conducted a series of exploratory data analyses (EDAs) using various visualization techniques to examine the data distributions. The following figure showcases some of the visualizations I created. During this process, I cleaned the data and developed an understanding of its distribution patterns, leading me to opt for a logarithmic transformation on the dataset. Furthermore, I performed some feature engineering tasks, such as one-hot encoding and normalization.

During the model training and prediction phase, I employed various algorithms such as polynomial regression, decision trees, random forests, AdaBoost, and others to construct an ensemble learning model. I used a soft voting approach to determine the final prediction results, thereby enhancing the model's generalization capability. This approach yielded an RMSE of less than 0.7 when applied to the logarithmically transformed dataset.

Description

In this project, we developed a 2D top-down pixel art-style game. This game incorporates elements of horror, adventure, and puzzle-solving, requiring players to uncover the story's truth step by step with the guidance of a narrator. And the most intriguing aspect is that, as players delve deeper into the game, they may discover that the narrator might not necessarily be on the player's side. This game will definitely offer players an experience that blends elements of Stanley Parable, Undertale, and Pokémon's Lavender Town. The game's development was based on Unity 2021.3.21f1, the artwork was created using Aseprite, and team collaboration was facilitated through GitHub and Trello. Feel free to explore our GitHub repo and download the demo of our game. Take a look at the trailer of our game below!

My Work

In this project, my main responsibility was to implement various game mechanics within Unity. Here, I will briefly list some of the key functions and mechanics I have implemented.

I created a dialogue system that includes the pop-up of dialogue boxes when the player interacts, scrolling text display, and synchronization with voice-over assets. Since this game is narrative-driven, the dialogue system is used frequently and holds significant importance.

Besides, I utilized 2D area effectors to create water flow effects, allowing the player to navigate through a maze-like cave scene while being carried by the current.

In addition, I accomplished the transitional effect of the player moving from land to swimming in the water. I also implemented the control and animation effects for the player's walking, swimming, and NPC movement.

Furthermore, I employed NavMesh to implement the NPC's automatic obstacle avoidance and pursuit of the player. When the player is caught by the NPC, the game declares a failure and displays a retry interface.
